## Abstract

The transition from linear, extractive industrial processes to a carbon-negative bioeconomy is fundamentally constrained by macroscopic thermodynamic bottlenecks: the energy penalty of water purification, the low aqueous solubility of , the latent heat of vaporization in biomass processing, and the biological lability of sequestered carbon. This thesis formalizes the engineering architecture of the "Symbiotic Factory," a closed-loop Water-Energy-Food-Carbon (WEFC) biorefinery. The system is compartmentalized into four interdependent unit operations mapped to elemental analogs: SUN (Plasmonic Interfacial Solar Desalination), WATER (Nano-bubbling Algal Cycloreactors), FIRE (Subcritical Hydrothermal Liquefaction and Z-Scheme Photocatalytic Fermentation), and TERRE (Polycyclic Aromatic Carbon Synthesis). By deriving the formal physical proofs and mass-energy balances for each module, we demonstrate that a cybernetically integrated system can bypass traditional thermodynamic penalties. The resulting architecture operates at theoretical limits, yielding an Energy Return on Investment (EROI)  and mathematically guaranteeing millennial-scale carbon dioxide removal (CDR).
